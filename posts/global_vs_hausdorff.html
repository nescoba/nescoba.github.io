A Global Dimension should be a Hausdorff dimension

General statement of the problem

The idea is this. Consider the main equation of Theorem 3.19 in this. Now consider Exercise 1.7.21 in this. They seem related, don't they?

Not immediatly, of course. You probably need to take logs in Theorem 3.19. Even if you do that, there is a coefficient that doesn't quite fit. But let me give you more profound reasons.

Cayley graphs of groups are metric spaces that look 'fractaly'. If graphed appropiatly, the Cayley graph of the free group looks like a fractal tree. And these fractals have interesting Hausdorff dimensions. In this particular instance, the ideal boundary with the shadow topology is the Cantor set, with Hausdorff dimension log 2 3.

On the other hand, modular categories feel a little like groups. Like finite ones, in a sense. The group of invertibles is literally a finite group. Finiteness is the whole point of a lot of these definitions.

And, moreover, you wouldn't be too simplistic if you just think of those categories as the representation categories of finite groups, because, in fact, they all are representation categories of Hopf algebras. finite dimensional ones, I think, but don't quote on that.

A way forward?

Consider a finite group, group. Let group.rep be its representation category and group.cayley its Cayley graph.

Maybe, a first question to ask is this. Given group, can I construct some metric space group.space, canonically related to group.rep, specially its graphic calculus? If so, are the metric properties of group.space somehow related to the metric properties of group.cayley? If so, does this construction have the property?

space.hausdorffDim = (group.rep).globalDim

What is this for?

Let cat be a fusion category, cat.gd its global dimension, obj a simple object with Frobenious-Perron dimension obj.fp

I have a couple of reasons behind thinking about this. The frist is that the definition of obj.fp seems a little disconnected from the rest of the theory, as does cat.gd. They play a huge role, and they have been explored in some depth, but they feel to me a little like misfit pieces of a puzzle. If this graph can be constructed, and obj.fp, cat.gd can be given some geometrical meaning, I would feel much better.

The second, a more specific application, is that maybe it helps give a sharper bound on the number of fusion categories with a given rank. Zhenghan mentioned in his talk that the argument in the proof of rank finiteness doen't give a good upper bound, because the number theoretical machinery behind it gives a ridiculously fast growing bound that isn't even good for rank 4 or something.
